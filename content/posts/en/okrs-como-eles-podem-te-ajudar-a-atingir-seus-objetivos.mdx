OKRs are generally known in the corporate world (Google, startups, etc.), but they can also be used in personal life. I myself struggled to maintain consistency in my goals until I discovered and applied OKRs.

It's not just a methodology—it's a way to bring focus and clarity to any life goal.

## What are OKRs?

The acronym **OKR** stands for Objectives and Key Results. This methodology was created in the 1970s by Andy Grove, former CEO of Intel, as a way to bring focus and alignment to the company during a period of strong growth. Later, John Doerr, who was a student of Grove, brought OKRs to Google in the late 1990s when the company had only about 40 people. From this success story, the practice became popular in various technology organizations like LinkedIn, Twitter, and Spotify, and today it's also being adapted for personal life.
But after all, what are OKRs?

In simple terms:

- **Objective (O):** where you want to go.
- **Key Results (KR):** how you measure if you're getting there.

An objective should be **inspiring and qualitative**, while key results need to be **measurable and concrete**. This combination ensures clarity: you know the direction and, at the same time, have objective criteria to evaluate progress.

A corporate example applied in a technology company could be:

**Objective:** Increase customer satisfaction.

- **KR1:** Raise NPS from 45 to 60 by the end of the quarter.
- **KR2:** Reduce average support response time from 24h to 12h.
- **KR3:** Achieve an 85% contract renewal rate.

Notice how the objective is broad and inspiring, but the KRs break this desire into clear indicators that can be tracked over time.

It's also important not to confuse OKRs with goals or tasks:

- **Objective:** is the destination.
- **KR:** is the progress milestone that shows if you're on track.
- **Task:** is the daily action that helps achieve the KR. Example: "respond to 10 tickets per day" is a task, not a key result.

More than a to-do list, OKRs carry their own philosophy:

- **Focus:** have few objectives, to direct energy on what matters.
- **Realistic ambition:** an OKR should challenge, but without being unattainable.
- **Transparency:** in companies, OKRs are public; in personal life, you can share with someone you trust to increase your commitment.
- **Progress, not perfection:** achieving 70% of an OKR can already be an excellent result, because it means you advanced consistently.

In summary, OKRs are a simple but powerful tool to transform a desire into a clear and measurable direction. And it's exactly this clarity that, as I'll show next, can change how we pursue our **personal goals**.

## But how can this help you in your personal life?

Up to this point, it seems clear how OKRs work within companies. But when we look at personal life, the logic is the same: **we, as individuals, also need clarity and purpose to maintain consistency in any journey.**

Think about common situations: starting an English course, trying to lose weight, saving money, reading more books, playing an instrument, or even improving personal relationships. How many times have we started something with excitement and, after a few weeks, the energy fades away? The problem isn't lack of will, but lack of **focus and progress criteria**.

And this is where OKRs come in. They connect what psychology calls **intrinsic motivation** (having a clear purpose) with **extrinsic motivation** (having milestones and indicators to track).

Studies on motivation and goals reinforce this importance:

- The _Goal-Setting Theory_ by **Locke & Latham** shows that clear and challenging goals significantly increase performance.
- The _Self-Determination Theory_ (Deci & Ryan) highlights that people need to feel purpose and progress to maintain engagement.
- Modern productivity books like _Atomic Habits_ (James Clear) and _Essentialism_ (Greg McKeown) also reinforce: small consistent actions, guided by clear objectives, generate lasting results.

When we apply OKRs to personal life, we stop depending solely on willpower. What was once a vague desire becomes a clear direction accompanied by concrete key results.

This clarity changes the game for three main reasons:

1. **Focus:** you choose what really matters and stop spending energy on everything at once.
2. **Consistency:** KRs work as checkpoints, keeping your motivation alive along the way.
3. **Real feedback:** by periodically reviewing your OKRs, you see where you're advancing and where you need to adjust course.

At its core, the value of OKRs in personal life lies in transforming distant objectives into something tangible, that can be tracked, measured, and celebrated.

## Sharing my personal OKRs

Alright, up to this point I've talked a lot about theory and examples aligned with the corporate scenario, but I want to open up a bit about my personal practice. After all, the title of this article is precisely about how OKRs can change the way you achieve your personal goals. And this isn't just a figure of speech.

As soon as I started experimenting with this methodology, I noticed I was falling into a pattern: I would get excited about various goals, create task lists (all beautiful on paper), but a month later I couldn't remember half of the things.

To make it more concrete, I'll share my OKRs at the time I'm writing this article. They represent different areas of my life and help me track my evolution process and direct effort where I want/need at this moment.

### My current OKRs

#### English – Advance to B2 level in the next 6 months, developing fluency and confidence through daily practice

- **KR1:** Have an attendance rate of at least 90% in classes with [Teacher Luana](https://www.instagram.com/teacherluapolinario/) and deliver 100% of weekly homeworks.
- **KR2:** Complete 24 conversation sessions by December/2025.
- **KR3:** Consume at least 36h of watched content in English by December/2025.

_**Why this OKR?** Because improving my English is one of my biggest goals this year. With well-defined KRs, I don't get lost in "study when I have time", because I have concrete metrics that remind me to practice every week._

#### Software Architecture – Expand my knowledge, leaving my comfort zone and applying learnings in practical initiatives (by June/2026)

- **KR1:** Complete critical reading of 3 key books (Fundamentals of Software Architecture, DDD, and Software Architecture: The Hard Parts) by June/2026.
- **KR2:** Produce at least 1 article per book on the blog, sharing the main learnings.
- **KR3:** Apply at least 2 learnings directly at work or in a personal project by June/2026.

_**Why this OKR?** Because I want to evolve beyond my core (front-end) and become more of a generalist in technology. The KRs force me to go beyond passive reading and transform knowledge into practice and content._

#### Knowledge contribution – Strengthen my positioning as a creator, sharing learnings with consistency (by December/2025)

- **KR1:** Publish at least 5 original articles on the blog by December/2025 and share each one on LinkedIn.
- **KR2:** Create and maintain a list of content ideas with at least 5 active topics.
- **KR3:** Evolve the blog (Next.js + MDX) with at least 3 improvements/features by December/2025.

_**Why this OKR?** Because I want to build a presence as someone who gives back to the world by sharing knowledge and thoughts. This requires both consistency in publications and evolution of the platform I use for it._

## Practical guide: How to create your own OKRs

After understanding the concept and seeing real examples, the natural question is: **how do I create my own OKRs?**

There's no single formula, but some guiding questions can help a lot in this process:

1. **Which areas of my life deserve an OKR?**  
   Not everything needs to become an OKR. Choose 2 to 4 areas that really matter at the moment (e.g.: health, studies, finances, career). This avoids dispersion and maintains focus.

2. **How many KRs are enough for each objective?**  
   Generally, 2 or 3 KRs per objective are enough. More than that can create confusion or a feeling of overload.

3. **What should be the review frequency?**  
   Reviewing is a fundamental part. You can track weekly (checking quick progress) and review more broadly every month or quarter. The important thing is not to let the OKR forgotten in limbo.

4. **Does it make sense to define specific activities and times?**  
   Yes, but remember: activities are not KRs. They are **means** to achieve results. For example: "take English class every Thursday" is an activity that helps achieve the KR "have an attendance rate of at least 90%".

5. **Are OKRs fixed or can they change?**  
   OKRs are not unbreakable contracts. They can and should be adjusted as your life evolves. The important thing is to have clarity about what makes sense today and courage to recalibrate when necessary.

### Common mistakes when creating personal OKRs

Even with good intention, it's easy to slip on some points. These are the mistakes I see most often (and that I've made myself):

- **Putting tasks in place of KRs.**  
  E.g.: "go to the gym 3 times a week" is not a KR, it's an activity. The true KR would be for example "reduce body fat % from 18% to 15%".

- **Having too many KRs.**  
  If you put 5 or 6 KRs for a single objective, you probably won't be able to track them all. Less is more.

- **Not reviewing periodically.**  
  Without tracking, the OKR loses its meaning. Reviewing is what transforms objective into continuous learning.

### And how to track all of this?

The good news is that it doesn't need to be anything complex. You can use a **simple spreadsheet**, a **Notion board**, or even a **physical notebook**. What matters is not the tool, but the consistency: reviewing periodically and keeping your objectives alive in daily life.

## Conclusion

OKRs changed the way I pursue my personal goals because they gave me **clarity, consistency, and flexibility**.  
Clarity to choose what really matters, consistency to track in a practical way, and flexibility to adjust course when necessary.

It's not about hitting 100% of the results, but about **advancing consistently**. And this progress, even if partial, is already transformative.

If you've never tried it, my suggestion is simple: choose an area of your life that makes a difference to you today, define an inspiring objective, write 2 or 3 measurable KRs, and track for a month.

The exercise itself will show you the power of this tool.
