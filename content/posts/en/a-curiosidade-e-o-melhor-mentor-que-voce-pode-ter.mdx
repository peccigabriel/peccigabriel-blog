Do you consider yourself a curious person? Yes, no, a little, maybe? Hmmm, I see!

And what if I told you that curiosity is a great ally of learning and knowledge—did you already know that?

I've been working with software development for about 10 years. Well, this might be a short time for some, a lot for others. But regardless, something became clear to me over time: there's a very strong consensus about the role of curiosity in the learning process (not just in technology).

> People whose curiosity is constantly evident have a more efficient learning system.

But what do I mean by _efficient_? Let's bring some science into our conversation.

## Curiosity and Learning: what science says

A study conducted by Dr. Matthias Gruber showed that it's no surprise we remember subjects that interest us more easily. However, when curiosity enters the scene, the impact is even greater: study participants had much more ease in remembering and learning.

According to Gruber, this happens because curiosity puts our brain in a motivational state, almost obsessive lol, to know, learn, and retain any type of information related to our curious focus. Additionally, a significant increase in **hippocampus** activity (the region linked to memory formation) was recorded, as well as in the circuit responsible for **pleasure and reward** sensations.

And this makes a lot of sense, right?

## How curiosity shaped my journey

A large part of what led me to work with technology has to do with curiosity. I remember when I was a child and received a computer from my grandmother. Since then, questions like:

- "Wow, how can I store data in this broken little box (aka hard drive)?"
- "How does the computer understand what I type and where the mouse pointer goes?"
- "How do I send a message on MSN and my friend receives it at his house?"

Yes, I searched for the answers. And you know what's cooler? Curiosity continues to push me to ask other questions, not just about technology, but about everything.

These questions turn into actions: studies, research, tests, proofs of concept. This creates a rewarding learning cycle. After all, who doesn't like discovering something new when they're truly curious?

## Curiosity: barrier or leverage?

At first glance, curiosity might seem challenging, almost a barrier. But this is a mistaken view. It should be seen as a **foundation for innovation and creativity**, a trigger to see possibilities, not obstacles.

Einstein himself, who deeply valued free thinking, said:

> _"I have no special talents. I am only passionately curious."_

Curious people tend to contribute creative and unexpected solutions. This applies to science, art, business, and even everyday relationships.

## The example of children

Have you ever noticed how children learn at impressive speed?

They are naturally curious beings: they ask, test, fail, try again… and learn.  
Curiosity is the engine of child development.

But as we grow up, we often lose this. Why?

It could be fear of making mistakes, fear of judgment, shame of asking, or simply lack of motivation.  
But the good news is: **curiosity can be reactivated.**

## Three ways to reignite your curiosity

**Normalize the discomfort of learning.**  
Learning something new can be uncomfortable because our brain seeks comfort. But curiosity and learning go hand in hand—accept this discomfort as a sign that you're expanding your limits.

**Be vulnerable.**  
Vulnerability is not weakness. It's accepting that you will make mistakes in the process, and that's okay. Being curious involves experimenting, failing, learning.

A book that changed my view on this was _Daring Greatly_ by Brené Brown. If you find it difficult to expose yourself to the new, I strongly recommend reading it.

**Connect with curious people.**  
Exchange ideas, participate in communities, talk about topics that intrigue you. Curiosity is also contagious and grows with the right environment.
